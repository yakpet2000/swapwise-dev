You Don't Have to Sell to Diversify.

There's a legal, IRS-recognized way to turn a concentrated stock position into a diversified portfolio — without triggering a tax bill. We'll show you how.

Who This Is For

Sound Familiar?

Millions of people are sitting on large stock positions they can't sell without a massive tax hit. If any of these sound like you, keep reading.

Long-Term Employee

You've accumulated company stock over years through RSUs, ESPP, or options. It's done well — but now 40%, 60%, or more of your net worth is in one ticker. Selling means a six-figure tax bill.

Inherited Stock

You inherited shares from a parent or grandparent. The stock has grown significantly since, and you'd like to spread the risk — but capital gains taxes make that expensive.

Concentrated Position

Maybe you bought early in a company that took off, or your portfolio has drifted over time. Either way, too much of your wealth depends on one company's future.

How It Works

A Legal Path to Diversification — Without Selling

Section 351 of the IRS tax code allows you to contribute stock into a new, diversified fund and receive shares of that fund in return — without owing taxes on the exchange. Here's the process, in plain English.

1

A New Fund Opens

A fund manager files to create a brand-new ETF and opens a window where investors can contribute stock before it launches.

2

You Contribute Your Stock

You transfer your appreciated shares — along with other investors doing the same — into the new fund. No sale happens, so no taxes are triggered.

3

You Receive ETF Shares

In exchange, you get shares of the new ETF equal in value to what you put in. You now own a diversified fund instead of a single stock.

4

Sell Whenever You Want

There's no lockup period on most 351 ETFs. You can hold long-term or sell ETF shares on the open market whenever it suits you.

⚠️

Important: This Isn't Risk-Free

To qualify, your contribution must pass IRS diversification rules — no single stock over 25% of what you contribute, and your top 5 can't exceed 50%. If your position is too concentrated, you may need to add other holdings to balance it out. Also, while this defers your taxes, it doesn't eliminate them — you'll owe capital gains when you eventually sell the ETF shares. We help you understand exactly where you stand before you commit.

Strategy Guide

Approaches by Position Size

The right strategy depends on how much stock you're working with. Here's a starting point based on typical position sizes.

Starter

Under $250K
  • Best fit for broad-market 351 ETFs with low minimums
  • Look for providers like Cambria or Alpha Architect that accept positions starting around $100K–$150K
  • Typically simple, low-cost index-style strategies (0.15%–0.30% expense ratios)
  • May need to add cash or additional positions to meet the 25/50 diversification test
  • Fewer provider options at this level — check exchange windows carefully
Most Common

Core

$250K – $2M
  • The sweet spot — most 351 ETF providers are designed for this range
  • Wide selection of strategies: core equity, global, factor-based, thematic
  • Enough size to diversify naturally and pass compliance tests more easily
  • Consider splitting across multiple exchange windows for added diversification
  • Tax savings typically range from $50K–$400K depending on embedded gains

Premium

$2M and Above
  • Access to both 351 ETFs and traditional exchange funds (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley)
  • Traditional exchange funds require 7-year lockup but have the longest IRS track record
  • May benefit from custom or white-label ETF structures
  • Higher fee tolerance opens options like Eagle Capital's concentrated value approach
  • Consider combining strategies — e.g., partial 351 ETF + partial exchange fund
